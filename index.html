93 million miles from our very homes, right in the middle of our solar system, is the sun – our very own star that brings us light and life. The sun affects weather here on Earth and determines whether our days will be filled with light and warmth, but it also affects the weather up above in space.

Sun

Weather in space isn’t made up of clouds, snow or rain. Instead, it is determined by the sun and the small particles it scatters towards Earth and the other planets in our solar system. We call this phenomenon solar wind.

Solar wind representation

It was just another day at the NASA headquarters when scientists slowly noticed that their radios weren’t working. They all scratched their heads in frustration wondering why they couldn’t listen to their favorite songs or hear their favorite news anchors. “Oh!” one shouted, “It must be a radio blackout storm!” These storms occur when explosions on the sun's surface cause particles to shoot through our Earth’s atmosphere, disturbing our radios here on Earth and preventing us from communicating with each other or listening to our favorite songs. This can be especially dangerous during emergency situations when we require radios to communicate important information. Fed up with the situation, the scientists decided they needed to do some work prompting them to build Little Parker, a solar probe that they’d launch up into space in hopes that he would someday touch the sun.
